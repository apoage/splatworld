# Light-Stable Orientation for Foliage Splat Clouds — Reformulated Research

Follow-up to the D7 shading report. This answers the three-part orientation prompt (patch-based labeling, photometric sign recovery, two-sided splat formats) and maps each finding onto milestones M-A…M-D. Companion to D7, which already established that runtime sign-agnostic shading makes all of this **non-blocking** — this is the "solve it anyway, for asymmetry quality" track.

## TL;DR
- **Patch-reduction is well-precedented; the anisotropic-splat criterion and the photometric vote are not.** Schertler et al. (CGF 2017) already does exactly "orient patches locally → one binary label per patch on a reduced graph → global solve" via MRF + QPBO, and Jakob et al. do it via parallel edge-collapse. So Q-B's *machinery* is off-the-shelf. What no one has published is (a) a segmentation criterion tuned for anisotropic surfels/leaf-faces, and (b) using **appearance** rather than a geometric flip-criterion as the per-patch vote.
- **The photometric sign cue (Q-C) is theoretically sound and biologically real, but genuinely unpublished for splats.** Calibrated photometric stereo *does* recover sign (the lit face is the one facing the known light); the front/back ambiguity is formally the **convex/concave (generalized bas-relief) ambiguity**, and specular asymmetry is a textbook disambiguator. Botany confirms adaxial vs abaxial surfaces differ measurably in reflectance (peak difference ~560–570 nm, i.e. in the green you already capture). But the only appearance-based *orientation* work uses cross-view **photo-consistency** (color agreement), not **shading asymmetry under known illumination**. Your specific cue — invert the render to recover the sign — appears to be new for splats. High risk, high reward, and it reuses machinery your decompose already half-owns.
- **Two-sided splats barely exist; the field either drops the normal or converts to a two-sided mesh.** BiGS and GS³ sidestep the whole problem with normal-free bidirectional/triple-Gaussian scattering ("normals are undefined"). LeafFit is the closest to per-face leaf handling — but it *leaves splat space*, segmenting leaves and baking an explicitly two-sided template **mesh** (duplicated front/back faces, split normals, separate textures). No published native two-sided/thickness-aware **splat** with per-face materials exists. The merged-face case is handled by *not representing it as a splat*.
- **There is no oriented-vegetation point-cloud benchmark.** Everyone evaluates on PCPNet / FamousShape / SceneNN (general shapes). PCPNet itself flags the two-sided-sheet sampling problem as a known failure. You will have to build your own eval — which is exactly what Q-D's runtime-observable metric is for.

## Key Findings

**1. Segment-then-label is the standard scalable formulation (Q-A/Q-B).** Reducing millions of per-point unknowns to thousands of per-patch labels on a reduced graph is precisely Schertler et al.'s streaming design, and the parallel edge-collapse of Jakob et al. does the same by merging graph regions. Both use **geometric** flip-criteria (Hoppe / Xie distance-weighted). Neither is tuned for anisotropic surfels, and both still assume the underlying surface is orientable — so they inherit the thin-double-sheet failure. The *reduction* is solved; the *foliage-specific criterion* and the *evidence source* are the open parts.

**2. The sign ≡ the convex/concave ambiguity, and known light breaks it (Q-C).** This reframes your problem into 40 years of shape-from-shading theory: front/back sign is the same degree of freedom as the hollow-face / GBR ambiguity, which **calibrated** photometric stereo resolves and **uncalibrated** does not. Since your decompose recovers the capture-time environment (its dominant SH lobe ≈ the sun), you are in the *calibrated* regime — the sign is in principle identifiable per splat from radiance. Specular and transmission asymmetry are independent disambiguators on top.

**3. Appearance-based orientation exists but not your version (Q-C novelty).** Photo-consistency has been used to orient/place points (Mendable Consistent Orientation; several MVS pipelines), and CVPR-2025 work solves a "sign field" from geometry. But *shading-asymmetry-under-known-illumination as a discrete front/back vote* is unpublished for splats. 3DGS inverse-rendering papers recover a continuous normal from an SDF gradient or a monocular prior, never a discrete sign from the appearance residual.

**4. Two-sided handling = drop the normal, or leave splat space (Q-3/representation).** The relighting field's answer to "the normal is undefined on thin/fuzzy geometry" is a normal-free scattering model (BiGS, GS³), not a two-sided splat. The foliage-asset field's answer is mesh conversion with explicit front/back faces (LeafFit). Nothing keeps splats *and* models two sides *and* relights.

**5. No vegetation-oriented benchmark; your runtime metric is the right call.** The absence of a foliage oriented-normal dataset means the only honest success signal is Q-D's temporal luminance stability of relit orbits — a runtime observable, not a GT-referenced angle.

## Details

### Q1 — Patch-based orientation as per-patch binary labels

**Closest precedent — Schertler, Savchynskyy & Gumhold, "Towards Globally Optimal Normal Orientations for Large Point Clouds" (Computer Graphics Forum 36, 2017; DOI 10.1111/cgf.12795).** This is the paper the reformulation is (unknowingly) re-deriving. Exact mechanism:
- **Formulation.** Normal propagation is recast as **energy minimization on a Markov Random Field**; the edge potentials come from a distance-weighted flip criterion (Hoppe's or Xie's). The energy is the negative-log of a labeling's probability, so the minimizer is the most probable consistent orientation.
- **Why it's hard.** The orientation problem is **submodular only in the trivial already-consistent case**; in general it is non-submodular, hence NP-hard. They therefore solve it approximately with **out-of-core QPBO** (quadratic pseudo-boolean optimization), which can leave some nodes unlabeled where evidence conflicts.
- **The patch reduction (your Q-B, exactly).** A streaming framework **orients patches locally, then solves one consistent orientation per patch on a reduced neighbour graph** — reported to reach quality similar to solving the full graph. This is the millions→thousands collapse you propose.
- **Segmentation criterion.** Patches are grown using the flip-criterion during the streaming pass (points in a segment share a color in their figures); the reduced graph then carries per-segment flip labels.
- **Conflict resolution.** They explicitly *allow contradictory* preliminary flips made during segmentation and let the global QPBO stage overrule them; separated components require **user-guided seed selection** (a documented limitation).
- **Relevance/limits for you.** It is *point-cloud-general*, uses *geometric* criteria only, and still assumes an orientable surface — it visualizes splat backsides but does not solve the two-sided-leaf case. It proves the reduction works; it does not give you a foliage criterion or an appearance vote.

**Parallel alternative — Jakob, Buchenau & Guthe (massively-parallel orientation via greedy edge collapse).** Uses a highly parallel **greedy edge collapse** that merges similar graph regions and orients them consistently — another route to the same patch-reduction, GPU-friendly, relevant if you want the reduction to scale to 2.4M on-device. (Title/venue not re-verified here — confirm before citing.)

**Newer — "A Divide-and-Conquer Approach for Global Orientation of Non-Watertight Scene-Level Point Clouds" (2025, arXiv 2505.23469).** Block-based (spatial divide-and-conquer) orientation targeting *scene-level, non-watertight* clouds — the most foliage-adjacent framing. But it states its own decisive limitation: it **may fail on points sampled from two extremely close surfaces, a limitation shared by all other methods.** That is your merged-leaf case.

**Foliage-specific segmentation precedent — LeafFit (Luo et al., Computer Graphics Forum 2026; DOI 10.1111/cgf.70374).** Not an orientation paper, but it *does* segment an unstructured foliage 3DGS into **per-leaf instances** (automatic + optional manual fallback), which is the natural "patch = leaf face" unit for Q-B. Its segmentation + non-rigid registration pipeline is the closest existing tooling for cutting a canopy splat cloud into leaf-level patches.

**Assessment for M-B.** The patch-label solver is a solved problem — adopt Schertler's MRF/QPBO (or Jakob's parallel collapse) wholesale. The two genuinely open sub-problems are: (i) a **segmentation criterion for anisotropic splats** — adjacency + normal-axis parallelism + scale continuity, closer to surfel region-growing than to isotropic k-NN; and (ii) whether real-canopy **patch graphs are connected enough** for propagation to add information over per-patch priors — likely *not*, since leaves are spatially disjoint, which is exactly why the per-patch **evidence** (Q-C) matters more than the propagation.

### Q2 — Photometric / appearance-based orientation (the novel cue)

**Theory: your sign is the convex/concave ambiguity.** Classical results map cleanly:
- **Calibrated photometric stereo (Woodham 1980)** recovers the true normal *including sign* when light directions are known — because the face oriented toward the light is brighter. With *known* illumination the front/back label is determined, not ambiguous.
- **Uncalibrated PS** determines normals only up to the **generalized bas-relief (GBR) ambiguity** (Belhumeur–Kriegman–Yuille), which under an integrability constraint collapses to the binary **convex/concave** ambiguity — i.e. *a sign flip*. The hollow-face illusion is this ambiguity made visible: a concave surface reads as convex under inverted light.
- **Disambiguators are well-studied.** Specularities reduce the ambiguity to a 2-DOF group (Drbohlav & Chantler, "Specularities Reduce Ambiguity of Uncalibrated Photometric Stereo") via the consistent-viewpoint constraint; polarization resolves convex/concave directly (shape-from-polarization-and-shading). These are exactly your Q-C cue #3 (specular response differs front/back) formalized.

**Consequence:** because your decompose already estimates the capture-time environment (dominant SH lobe ≈ sun), you are effectively in the *calibrated* regime, where the sign is **statistically identifiable per splat** from the lit/backlit radiance asymmetry — precisely Q-C's premise. Aggregating the vote per patch (Q-B) trades single-splat noise for many samples per unknown, which is the right move given how noisy per-splat radiance is.

**Biology confirms the asymmetry is real and in-band.** Hyperspectral studies of leaves report the **adaxial (top) and abaxial (bottom) surfaces differ measurably in reflectance**, with the largest visible-band difference around **560–570 nm** (macadamia; similar for avocado, where either surface even predicts foliar nutrients). Mechanistically this is cuticle/wax vs stomata/pubescence — your Q-C cue #1 (tops vs undersides differ in albedo/sheen). So the front/back label has a *material* signature, not only a shading one; a per-splat albedo/sheen asymmetry is independently informative even before lighting is considered.

**Existing appearance-based orientation — the honest precedents (and why they're not your cue):**
- **"Mendable Consistent Orientation of Point Clouds"** — orients using a cost based on **photo-consistency** (cross-view color agreement), extensible to multi-view. This *is* appearance-driven orientation, but photo-consistency measures color *agreement across views* (a geometry/position cue), not *shading asymmetry under a known light* (a sign cue). It answers "where is the surface," not "which face is lit."
- **MVS photo-consistency pipelines** (e.g. USPTO 11055902) likewise use cross-view color variance to place points along the normal — position, not sign.
- **"Consistent Normal Orientation via Least Squares on Delaunay tetrahedra" (Fu et al., CVPR 2025)** solves a **sign field** whose gradient flips normals — relevant to your D7 coarse-voxel sign-field idea — but purely geometric, and it explicitly breaks when a Delaunay cell spans **two parallel sheets** (your merged-leaf case again).
- **3DGS inverse-rendering (GaussianShader, R3DG, GI-GS, DPIR, 3DSS, GAINS, etc.)** recover the normal as an **SDF gradient** or from a **monocular normal prior**, never as a discrete sign decoded from the appearance residual.

**Verdict on novelty (the deliverable asks for this explicitly): the Q-C cue is genuinely unpublished for splats.** "Use multi-view radiance asymmetry under the recovered capture illumination to assign a discrete front/back label per splat or per leaf-patch" is not something any surveyed point-cloud, photogrammetry, or inverse-rendering-GS paper does. The *ingredients* are all published (calibrated PS sign recovery; specular disambiguation; adaxial/abaxial reflectance asymmetry); the *combination for splat orientation* is new. Caveat: this is a negative existence claim over a large literature — treat it as "no precedent found after a broad search," not a proof.

### Q3 — Two-sided / thickness-aware splat representations

**Baseline: 2DGS surfels are single-normal.** A 2D Gaussian carries `n = (t_u × t_v)/‖t_u × t_v‖` — **one** normal per surfel, sign from the cross-product handedness. A single 2DGS surfel *is* the merged-face representation; its "back" is just `−n`. 2DGS and its inverse-rendering descendants have no per-face materials.

**Strategy A — drop the normal (the relighting field's actual answer):**
- **BiGS: Bidirectional Gaussian Primitives for Relightable 3D Gaussian Splatting (arXiv 2408.13370).** Uses a light- and view-dependent **bidirectional spherical-harmonic** scattering model and states plainly that it uses **no surface-normal-dependent reflectance function**, which suits Gaussian splatting **where the normals are undefined**. Built for fur/translucent/volumetric appearance from OLAT data; real-time relighting. This is the material-level version of D7's "make the sign irrelevant."
- **GS³: Efficient Relighting with Triple Gaussian Splatting (SIGGRAPH Asia 2024, arXiv 2410.11419).** Notes that normal-based methods **require well-defined surface normals**, limiting them to opaque objects with clear boundaries; replaces the normal-BRDF with learnable spherical-Gaussian lobes. Again: no sign needed.

**Strategy B — leave splat space, bake a two-sided mesh (the foliage-asset field's answer):**
- **LeafFit: Plant Assets Creation from 3D Gaussian Splatting (Luo et al., CGF 2026).** The most on-point paper for per-face leaves. It observes implicit-surface conversion **thickens thin sheets and can't give front/back different textures**, then instead: segments the 3DGS into leaf instances → non-rigid-registers to a template → extracts a **thin two-sided template mesh**, rendering the leaf from front and back and **duplicating each triangle into a front/back pair with split normals and separate UVs**. Result is a single-sided-primitive but genuinely two-sided asset. Handling of the merged-face case = it doesn't keep the splat; the template mesh *is* the thickness-resolved two-sided surface.

**Native two-sided/thickness-aware splat with per-face materials: none found.** No published splat/surfel format stores two materials for the two faces of a thin sheet and relights them. The closest conceptual precedents are all mesh-side (two-sided BSDFs, UE "Two Sided Foliage" which auto-flips the normal per face and adds a Subsurface Color transmission tint) — covered in D7. This is a real gap: a *thickness-aware two-sided surfel* is unbuilt.

**Datasets / benchmarks for oriented vegetation clouds: none.** Oriented-normal evaluation universally uses **PCPNet**, **FamousShape**, **ABC**, and **SceneNN** — general CAD/objects/indoor, no vegetation category. Notably **PCPNet's own paper** flags the failure mode you care about: a point cloud that **samples two sides of a thin sheet** makes it hard to tell which side a point came from, and oriented-normal error is dominated by orientation flips that "only [work] well for extremely small noise levels." So the literature both lacks a foliage benchmark *and* documents why one would be hard to label — reinforcing Q-D's runtime-observable metric as the pragmatic choice.

## Recommendations — mapped to milestones

**M-A (two-sided classifier + schema flag) — do it, it's cheap and honest.**
- **Class (iii) detector.** Flag a splat `two_sided` when `s_min` exceeds a local sheet-thickness estimate. Precedent for the geometric signal: the **shape-diameter function** (used in the CVPR-2025 sign-field paper to gauge local thinness) and **Demantké planarity** `(λ₂−λ₃)/λ₁` from the Gaussian's own scale eigenvalues (D7, Q5). A merged-two-face splat reads as *thin-in-min-axis but not cleanly planar*, and the "two-parallel-sheets in one cell" test is the exact geometric signature to borrow.
- **Deliverable:** per-asset populations of class (i)/(ii)/(iii), and the schema honestly carrying the flag instead of a fabricated sign. This directly feeds the D7 runtime confidence gate.

**M-B (patch segmentation + within-patch metric) — adopt the solved solver, build the foliage front-end.**
- **Solver:** Schertler MRF + QPBO on the reduced per-patch graph (or Jakob's parallel edge-collapse if you want GPU scale). Don't reinvent this.
- **Segmentation front-end (the open bit):** grow patches by adjacency + normal-axis parallelism + scale continuity on anisotropic splats; LeafFit's leaf-instance segmentation is the closest reusable tooling. Expect canopy patch graphs to be **poorly connected** (disjoint leaves), so plan for propagation to add little — which is *why* M-C matters.
- **Metric (Q-D):** within-patch opposition → 0 (exclude patch-boundary pairs and `two_sided` splats). Pair it with the runtime observable.

**M-C (photometric sign vote) — the novel, high-value prototype. Prioritize if asymmetry quality matters.**
- **Formulation:** treat it as *inverting the render for the sign*. Per splat/patch, given the per-view radiance samples and the recovered sun (calibrated-PS regime), score the two sign hypotheses by which better explains the lit/backlit radiance — the lit face brighter, the backlit face carrying the transmission glow (your D7 transmission term supplies the backlit prediction). Add the specular-asymmetry and adaxial/abaxial-albedo cues as independent votes; aggregate per patch (Q-B) to beat per-splat noise.
- **Why it's worth trying:** it is the *only* cue that could recover the true label on class-(ii) leaf splats, where all geometric methods are at their ~50% floor, and it reuses per-view residuals your decompose already computes.
- **Risk:** genuinely unpublished for splats → no baseline to copy; identifiability may be weak per-splat and only emerge per-patch; sensitive to sun-estimate error and interreflection. Prototype on **one asset, offline, patch-level** before any scaling — exactly as the milestone scopes it.

**M-D (A/B vs runtime-agnostic baseline) — the only trustworthy verdict.** Since no oriented-vegetation benchmark exists, measure the **visual delta** of resolved-sign shading (true one-sided foliage + top-lit/bottom-lit transmission asymmetry) against the D7 sign-agnostic baseline, via the lighting-stability harness + owner eyeball. If the asymmetry delta is small, stop — D7 already ships. If it's large on hero assets, M-C earns its place.

**Overall verdict.** The reformulation is correct and the milestones are well-ordered. Concretely: **M-A and M-B are low-risk and mostly assembly of existing methods** (Demantké/shape-diameter classification; Schertler/Jakob patch solver; LeafFit segmentation). **M-C is the one genuinely new contribution** and the only path to physically-correct transmission asymmetry — it's unpublished, moderately risky, and reuses your inverse-rendering machinery, so it's the right thing to *prototype small and gate on M-D* rather than commit to blindly. Do **not** expect any of it to beat D7's runtime-agnostic shading on the *average* splat; its entire value is on the class-(ii) leaf-face subset and the asymmetry it unlocks.

## Caveats
- **The Q-C novelty claim is a "no precedent found," not a proof.** I searched point-cloud orientation, photogrammetry, inverse-rendering GS, photometric stereo, and plant phenotyping; the shading-asymmetry-under-known-light sign vote for splats did not appear. It remains possible something exists under different terminology (e.g. in remote sensing or a non-English venue).
- **Schertler's exact segmentation/conflict details are from abstracts, figure captions, and secondary summaries**, not a full read of §4–5. Before implementing, pull the Heidelberg PDF (hci.iwr.uni-heidelberg.de/vislearn/…/schertler-orientation-cgf2015-with-supplement.pdf) for the precise flip-criterion, seed-selection, and QPBO-unlabeled handling.
- **Jakob et al. — exact title/venue not re-verified here;** cited via secondary references ("parallel … edge collapse … merges similar parts of the graph and orients them consistently"). Confirm the citation before use.
- **Calibrated-PS "sign is determined" is a clean-Lambertian statement.** On real leaves with interreflection, translucency, specular sheen, and an *estimated* (not measured) sun, the per-splat sign will be probabilistic, not certain — which is exactly why per-patch aggregation and a confidence, not a hard label, is the right output.
- **Adaxial/abaxial reflectance figures (~560–570 nm) are from hyperspectral leaf studies under controlled illumination**, not from splat captures; they establish that the cue *exists* in-band, not its SNR in your pipeline.
- **LeafFit and the 2025 divide-and-conquer paper are very recent (2025–2026);** details may shift, and I've read abstracts/figure text rather than full methods. BiGS/GS³ specifics are likewise from abstracts and related-work sections.
- **No claim that a native two-sided splat is impossible** — only that none is published. It may be the actual research contribution hiding in M-A's schema work (a surfel carrying two per-face albedos + a thickness), which would be novel in its own right.
